Recipe

Castella

Description

Castella (カステラ, kasutera) is a kind of soft sponge cake that orginated in Japan, brought in the 16th century via Portuguese merchants, so it's name is derived from the Portuguese phrase "Pão de Castela" (“bread from Castille"). It is a specialty of Nagasaki, as at the time, it was the only port open to foreign trade. The cake is baked in square or rectangular molds and cut into individual slices. It gets it's volume from whipped eggs and uses a sugar syrup called mizuame, a sweetener for wagashi, and it can also be flavored with other things, such as matcha or honey. There is also a popular Taiwanese variant that was introduced from Japan during its rule over the island, and it has more of a souffle texture.

Ingredients

  • 7 room temperature eggs
  • 1 1/2 cups (200g) bread flour
  • 1 1/4 cups (250g) sugar
  • 1/3 cup (80g) honey
  • 1/4 cup (60g) water or milk
  • optional flavors:
    • matcha - 2 teaspoons matcha powder
    • chocolate - 1 tablespoon cacao powder
    • strawberry - 1 tablespoon strawberry puree

Equipment

  • whisk/stand mixer/hand mixer (for beating eggs)
  • 9" x 9" (23cm x 23cm) square baking tray
  • parchment paper
  • sieve

Prep Time: 25 minutes

Cook Time: 50 minutes

Instructions

  1. Preheat oven to 356F (180C). Line cake pan with parchment paper.
  2. Place eggs in a stand mixer (or bowl) and beat until the egg mixture has frothed up and thickened (4-10 minutes), slowly adding the sugar in in equal parts as it beats.
  3. Stir milk (or water) and honey together in a separate bowl and heat to lukewarm until the honey is dissolved.
  4. Sift the bread flour and set aside.
  5. Add half of the milk and honey mixture to the eggs and mix by hand with a spatula until incorporated. Add half of the bread flour and repeat the mixing process. Repeat with the rest of the milk and honey mixture, then the rest of the bread flour.
  6. Pour the cake batter into the pan and tap to remove air bubbles. Bake for 10 minutes, then lower the temperature to 320F (160C) and bake for another 30-40 minutes.
  7. Take the cake out from the oven and drop the pan onto a solid, flat surface from a height of about 5" (12.5cm) to release the air from the cake to avoid collapsing.
  8. Spread plastic wrap on a flat surface, and flip the cake upside down and out of the pan onto the plastic to let cool. Wrap it with the plastic after it has completely cooled.

Recipe Websites

Delish

I enjoy Delish's site because it has their ingredients side by side with the directions to avoid scrolling repeatedly to check for measurements. I also like that they have a "Jump to Recipe" button, as it makes it easier for someone who may not be interested in reading/watching any of the extra information on the page. I also like that it clearly outlines the yield, prep, and total time at the beginning so that the user knows the parameters before getting started. It also provides a small poll that allows for interactivity.

NYT Cooking

I like that NYT Cooking also has their ingredients set side by side with the cooking instructions, so I don't have to scroll up and down repeatedly to check for ingredient measurements as I progress through the recipe. It also has a function for ingredient substitution, which I think is important considering the amount of peopole that might need to accomodate food allergies/sensitivites or diet accomodations. I also like that is allows for reviews/5-star rating.

Jamie Oliver

Jamie Oliver's site is nice as it provides very detailed nutrition information in addition to serving sizes, time, and difficulty information. It recommends other recipes at the bottom, and the directions are separated into smaller, managable steps rather than being shoved all into one step. The presentation of the recipes as a collection also makes good use of icons and information a user might want to know before clicking into a recipe.
